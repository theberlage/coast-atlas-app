## Coast Atlas

_Coast Atlas_ investigates and documents the physical transformation of a section of the South Holland coastline, from Hoek van Holland to Katwijk aan Zee. Based on archival research, it analyzes territorial developments and their relationship with the coastal landscape, infrastructure, and architecture, connecting it to the sociocultural history of the Netherlands. With the aid of digital tools such as Allmaps—an open-source platform for curating, georeferencing, and exploring digitized maps—the atlas explores the possibility to overlay, compare, and annotate the coastal landscape’s spatial developments over the past century, while also allowing quick relation of sources at different scales.

Documentation is developed into arguments that identify architectural and landscape design questions related to territorial developments in the Dutch coastal area, and approaches taken in response. These arguments are articulated through a set of drawings, which are digitized and georeferenced on a collective map depicting the studied section of the South Holland coastline, linking physical and digital scales from territory to space. The collective map, generated by digitally layering fragments from diverse archival maps—including national and international sources serving various purposes such as topographic, military, and site plans— creates a unique narrative of coastal evolution over time.

Focusing on the period from the 1860s to the 1960s, Coast Atlas covers a century during which the relatively untouched Dutch coastal landscape underwent significant transformations through cultivation and thorough rationalization. The work is examined through three thematic lenses, looking at the coast as a dune landscape, a bathing site and a defense line.

Over this period, the dune landscape has been extensively transformed by human intervention, serving multiple critical functions including coastal protection, infrastructure development, and drinking water supply.

The construction of the first bathhouse in Scheveningen marked the arrival of seaside recreation in the Netherlands, initially as a form of
healthcare and leisure for the upper class. Later, the opening of the first campsite in Hoek van Holland in 1924 introduced coastal leisure to the working class. The architecture’s materiality reflects the values embedded in its design, mirroring the evolving social values in Dutch society.

During World War II, the coast transformed into a military defense line, as it became part of the Nazi Germany’s _Atlantikwall_. The Dutch coastline featured numerous fortifications, bunkers, and artillery positions, reflecting the strategic importance of the region. World War II left its marks on the coastal landscape, while also accelerating its transformation through numerous reconstruction efforts. Leisure time for the masses triggered widespread creation of recreational landscapes, enhanced by postwar economic growth.

With the 1955 Delta Plan, further control over the seafront was seized, yet an increasing consciousness of environmental concerns also
heralded new approaches towards the design and regulation of the coastal environment.

_Coast Atlas is the third iteration of the Berlage’s long-standing Project NL design-research course in collaboration with Allmaps and the TU Delft Library. The project is arranged in three parts: first, collecting, categorizing, and annotating archival materials to document the spatial changes related to the specific themes; second, analyzing and translating these transformations into synthetic drawings, developing arguments that identify architectural and landscape design questions related to territorial developments in the Dutch coastal area, and approaches taken in response; and third, summarizing the findings through a physical exhibition, a digital application, a publication, and a final event. This comprehensive approach allows students to engage with the Dutch culture of shaping the environment, particularly focusing on coastal transformations over time._

The source code of this website can be found on [GitHub](https://github.com/theberlage/coast-atlas-app). Coding by Jules Schoonman and Bert Spaan.
